What can you learn from primary sources about the COVID-19 lab-leak hypothesis?

Sun 20 June 2021

I'm going to break with my regular newsletter format (and conveniently ignore the fact that I have not posted for several months) and share with you a few thoughts I have about the COVID-19 lab-leak hypothesis.

You might remember that, in an earlier newsletter, I posted a link to a story about the drug hydroxychloroquine and how political its use became once it received support from Trump. It seems a similar thing happened with the lab-leak hypothesis. My intention today is not to provide commentary around whether I think the lab-leak hypothesis is true or not — I think it's likely enough that it is worth investigating but no more than that — instead it is to show what you can learn from primary source material.

In a politically polarised media landscape it can be hard to discern truth from fiction. Every fact is filtered, and often distorted, through the narrative spin that journalists wish to put on the underlying facts.

Today I wish to present you with some facts that are often cited but may not be as salient if not verified first hand.

The four facts I would like to present are:

  1. Furin cleavage sites have been identified as being a mechanism for increased infectiousness of coronavirus in humans
  2. A researcher from the Wuhan Institute of Virology (WIV), Shi Zhengli, in joint work with Ralph Baric engineered a chimeric virus with increased infectivity in 2015.
  3. Ralph Baric has recently signed his name to a statement declaring that the lab-leak hypothesis should not be ruled out
  4. Peter Daszak, a proponent of the natural origin hypothesis, remarks in a podcast released in December 2019 referencing the ability to modify viruses in the lab and mentioning Ralph Baric by name

Furin cleavage sites as a mechanism for increased infectivity in humans

Several papers have been published that demonstrate that adding a furin cleavage site to a coronavirus increases its infectiousness. One example is a paper entitled Activation of the SARS coronavirus spike protein via sequential proteolytic cleavage at two distinct sites.

A key quote from the paper is:

We also introduced a furin cleavage site at both the S2′ cleavage site within S2 793-KPTKR-797 (S2′), as well as at the junction of S1 and S2. Introduction of a furin cleavage site at the S2′ position allowed trypsin-independent cell–cell fusion, which was strongly increased by the presence of a second furin cleavage site at the S1–S2 position. Taken together, these data suggest a novel priming mechanism for a viral fusion protein, with a critical proteolytic cleavage event on the SARS-CoV S protein at position 797 (S2′), acting in concert with the S1–S2 cleavage site to mediate membrane fusion and virus infectivity.

Another paper called Furin Cleavage Site Is Key to SARS-CoV-2 Pathogenesis published on 26 August 2020 provided evidence that the furin cleavage site in SARS-CoV-2 (which causes COVID-19) was key to its infectiousness

Chimeric virus expressing a modified spike protein created by Shi Zhengli and Ralph Baric

In 2015 Shi Zhengli in joint work with Ralph Baric modified the spike protein of an existing virus in order to make it more infectious.

Paper: A SARS-like cluster of circulating bat coronaviruses shows potential for human emergence

I would like to point out the editor's note

We are aware that this article is being used as the basis for unverified theories that the novel coronavirus causing COVID-19 was engineered. There is no evidence that this is true; scientists believe that an animal is the most likely source of the coronavirus.

It correctly asserts that the paper is not evidence of a lab origin for SARS-CoV-2. The paper is not evidence of anything except the fact that tools and techniques exist to readily modify existing viruses to increase their infectivity, and that both Shi Zhengli and Ralph Baric have knowledge of these techniques, and access to the resources necessary to apply them.

The key quote from the paper is:

Therefore, to examine the emergence potential (that is, the potential to infect humans) of circulating bat CoVs, we built a chimeric virus encoding a novel, zoonotic CoV spike protein—from the RsSHC014-CoV sequence that was isolated from Chinese horseshoe bats in the context of the SARS-CoV mouse-adapted backbone. The hybrid virus allowed us to evaluate the ability of the novel spike protein to cause disease independently of other necessary adaptive mutations in its natural backbone. Using this approach, we characterized CoV infection mediated by the SHC014 spike protein in primary human airway cells and in vivo, and tested the efficacy of available immune therapeutics against SHC014-CoV. Together, the strategy translates metagenomics data to help predict and prepare for future emergent viruses.

Ralph Baric has recently signed an open letter suggesting further investigation of the lab leak hypthesis

Ralph Baric, who was the scientist that co-engineered a virus with Shi Zhengli in 2015, is one of 19 scientists that recently signed an open letter published in Science imploring the scientific community not to rule out the lab leak hypothesis saying "We must take hypotheses about both natural and laboratory spillovers seriously until we have sufficient data".

The article in Science can be found here: Investigate the origins of COVID-19.

Peter Daszak comments on Ralph Baric's work in podcast released in December 2019

In an episode of This Week in Virology, released on 19 May 2021 Peter Daszak is interviewed. At around the 29m50s mark he remarks that viruses can be easily manipulated in the lab and that spike proteins "drive a lot of what happens" in coronaviruses. Yet he has been a big proponent of the zoonotic origin theory (which make still be the correct one).

Here's a link to the clip on YouTube.

Conclusion

Both the lab-leak and zoonotic origin hypotheses have evidence in favour of them but neither has conclusive evidence that makes it clearly correct. At this point we simply don't have the evidence to rule out either one and, as such, both should remain on the table.

I would urge people to try their hand at digging through primary source material when reading about this particular issue and for similarly "politically hot" topics. It's quite remarkable what you can find with only a modicum of searching on the Internet. Many claims made in newspaper articles can be decisively debunked merely by doing a bit of reading of primary source material.
