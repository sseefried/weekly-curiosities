Finite sums for infinite divergent sequences

Srinivasa Ramanujan was probably one of the most interesting mathematicians of the 20th century. He grew up in a poor family in a remote part of India. Not only was he not provided with a high quality mathematical education but — after he had defied the odds and developed a prodigious ability &mdash he had no one around him that could even recognise his achievements.

At the age of 26 he wrote a letter to the famous mathematician G. H. Hardy. Hardy was not put off by Ramanujan's lack of formal education and immediately recognised his ability and originality. The Hollywood trope of the established professional that can't recognise genius and only cares for credentials did not hold in this case.

One of the results Ramanujan submitted in the letter concerned a summation of ordinary, whole numbers (i.e. the _natural numbers_ in mathematics). He made the claim that if you broke one of the rules of summing infinite series, you would actually get a finite and more astonshingly, a negative number as a result!

Where as you would expect the following to be true

1 + 2 + 3 + … = ∞

Ramanujan posited that, if you broke just one rule, the result is actually

1 + 2 + 3 + … = -1/12

What? Negative one twelfth!?

Watch the video below for a fascinating explanation of how you can derive this result yourself. You only need a smattering of algebra to understand the result, and it's arguable if you even need that.

A framework for dieting

There are a plethora of diets you can try to follow, but many are just small variations on others, and it's not always obvious what the underlying principles of the diet are. e.g. how does it work?, what kind of body chemistry does it hope to promote? does it involve calorie restriction or not?

The field of nutrition is full of nonsense but not all of it is nonsense. Clearly there is such a thing as a good diet even if we don't know exactly what it is. We certainly know there is such a thing as a bad diet.

If we were to liken it to the field of medicine I'd say we're still at the stage where we think cholera is caused by miasmas, leeches are a considered a reasonable therapy, and doctors still don't wash their hands. That is, we have a long way to go until we really know just what constitutes a healthy diet and what doesn't. David Chapman wrote a polemic outlining just how poor the knowledge base of nutrition really is.

However, todays video was made by Peter Attia who trying to change this dismal situation. While he doesn't offer any answers in the video below, what he does is present a framework for categorising diets that helps navigate the world of dieting.

One part of the video I particular enjoyed is where is tells us that the standard American diet is precisely what you get when you optimise food production to be the following four things:

  • cheap
  • tasty
  • scalable. i.e. mass produced and easily transported to the consumer
  • portable i.e. easily carried by the consumer
