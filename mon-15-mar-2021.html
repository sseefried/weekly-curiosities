Finite sums for infinite divergent sequences

Srinivasa Ramanujan was probably one of the most interesting figures in mathematics in the 20th century if for his back-story alone. He grew up as part of a poor family, in a part of India where, not only was he not provided with a high quality mathematical education but — after he had defied the odds and developed a prodigious ability — had no one around him that could even recognise his achievements.

At the age of 26 he wrote a letter to the famous mathematician G. H. Hardy. Unlike the trope one seens in many movies, Hardy was not put off by Ramanujan's lack of formal education and immediately recognised his ability and originality.

One of the results present in the paper had to do with summing the so-called natural numbers which are just the ordinary (whole) counting numbers we use. He made the claim that if you broke one of the rules of summing infinite series, you would actually get a finite and negative result!

We would all expect that the following is true

1 + 2 + 3 + … = ∞

But Ramanujan posited that, if you broke just one rule, the result is actually

1 + 2 + 3 + … = -112
