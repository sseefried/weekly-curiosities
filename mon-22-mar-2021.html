An interview with the author of Brave New World

Aldous Huxley published a famous novel titled Brave New World outlining a dystopian future very different from George Orwell's 1984 and one that, in my opinion, more accurately reflects the dystopian elements of our society. There's even a nice comic comparing the two literary works. It was also recently made into a television series which, although departing from the plot of the original book, does manage to capture the general feel of the society depicted in the book very well.

Although Brave New World was published in 1932 the interview I'm linking to was done in 1958. The world had changed enormously. There had even been a world war in the intervening time yet Huxley felt that his dystopian vision was even closer to being realised than when he originally wrote the novel.

One part of the interview I particularly liked had to do with his criticism of Madison Avenue's brand of advertising. Huxley believed it bypassed our rational faculties by appealing directly to our more base desires. This was a catastrophe from his point of view as he believed one of the important foundations one required for a healthy democracy was that people made conscious decisions as opposed to unconscious ones. I wonder what he would make of the even more crafty advertising of today that not only bypasses our critical faculties but is tailor made for our individual predilections.

The Reality of Being Poor-ish

People have this very deep seated intuition that work should be paid in proportion to the difficulty of the job. Coming to the realisation and acceptance that it doesn't work this way is, I think, an important part of one's self development and even empowerment.

In reality people are paid in proportion to the rarity and perceived value of the skill they are providing. This is why a kitchen worker can work themselves to the bone, be treated awfully by their superiors, and still be paid a pittance while a highly skilled lawyer can charge so much for each hour of their time. The motto of the story here is: try not to be replaceable and try not specialise in a skill that is perceived as low value.

But even this formulation isn't the full story. I was struck by some additional information in this quote from the article.

The worst treatment you tend to get is, perhaps counterintuitively, in jobs that approach living wage despite your low value and replaceability as a worker. These jobs tend towards needing someone who can put in a lot of hours for customer interactions that require a single point of contact; they reward intense focus over long periods of time in unpleasant situations. Another way to say this is that these are high-stress jobs with a lot of mandatory overtime and very fast pacing - they pay a lot because they can’t get people to do them otherwise. Turnover in these jobs is absurd despite the pay - some people can keep up with them but it isn’t typical. In my first (and hopefully last) claims adjuster job, two people had heart attacks in my office in a six month period.

This provides a nod to the idea that replaceability and low value are determining factors in what you are paid (and how you are treated) but that there is another factor at play. Some jobs, in addition, require high responsibility which is a bargaining chip increased wages. However, as the quote above makes clear, the worst combo appears to be high responsibility coupled with highly replaceable/low value work.

The rest of the article is also great and refreshingly free of jealousy towards rich people! It outlines how poverty creates all sorts of negative feedback loops in your life making it hard to escape from. I found the part about the ever-looming spectre of car repairs riveting. It is well worth a read.

F-16's computer pulls out of a 9.1G dive and saves pilot

In this video you see an F-16's Automatic Ground Collision Avoidance System do exactly that and save a pilot's life. Within the space of only a few seconds he drops from 15000ft to 4000ft. In fact, the pilot blacked out because of the G forces and would certainly have perished without the assistance of system.
