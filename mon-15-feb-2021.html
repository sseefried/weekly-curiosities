Crypto-chernobyl

You might have heard that Bitcoin uses a lot of electricity. The reason for this is quite technical but the short story is that Bitcoin relies on all the people in the network to come to agreement on the transactions in the ledger. An ingenious system called mining ensures that blocks of transactions can only be stamped as authentic by solving a difficult maths problem. This is also known as Proof of Work. Miners receive a significant reward for authenticating blocks of transactions. At the time of writing this is 12.5 bitcoins or about USD$600,000.

As more miners enter the Bitcoin network the difficulty of solving the mathematical problem is increased. This is necessary for technical reasons I just don't have space to go into in this newsletter, but the downside is that more and more of the world's electricity is consumed. Worse still, the amount of electricity that Bitcoin could use is unbounded.

Bitcoin was (and is) a bold experiment and its designers probably didn't see this coming but it's become a huge problem. The combined electricity consumption of all Bitcoin miners recently exceeded that of Argentina, a country of 45 million people.

This leads us to the article I'd like to share. It's a polemic against this aspect of Bitcoin and makes the case that Bitcoin is an environmental and moral disaster because of this design flaw.

Passive funds are distorting the markets

So here's a question I've always wanted answered. Why does superannuation work? After all, markets only work when investors inject information into the system by buying stocks they think will succeed and selling ones they think will fail. What happens when you simply leave these choices to other people? Can the market work as it is supposed to? Will it not suffer because of the smaller number of people who are actually making decisions about what to buy and sell?

It turns out that my suspicions are well founded although the explanation is definitely way above my pay grade. Superannuation, index funds, managed funds, and many other kinds of investment vehicles are examples of what is known as passive investing. The defining characteristic of them is that you're leaving the choice of what to invest in to someone else. The following video goes into why passive investing is distorting the market, and incidentally, explains why hedge funds have become more susceptible to "short squeezes" caused by rallies like the infamous GameStop rally of the last few weeks.
